The Merkabit Quantum Computer

Hardware designed around the architecture reality is computed in.

Native ternary. Eisenstein lattice. Five-gate primitive set. Self-sustaining tesseract memory. Closed-cycle verification.

Modern quantum computers are kludges — binary qubits arranged in square grids, programmed through gate decompositions that emulate richer physics the hardware was not designed to express. The Merkabit Quantum Computer matches its architecture to the underlying physics: a ternary register on a hexagonal lattice, a five-gate primitive set that closes into a self-verifying cycle, and a memory geometry that sustains coherence for ten Coxeter periods rather than collapsing in microseconds.

The merkabit — dual-spinor ternary register

Substrate

Eisenstein lattice ℤ[ω]

2D hexagonal-triangular lattice formed by the Eisenstein integers, where ω is a primitive sixth root of unity. Six nearest neighbours per cell (vs four on square grids). Native Z₃ structure — every triangle is one of three orientations. Carries the framework's E₆ Coxeter symmetry.
